Find your own PATH

How will you set yourself up for
success after high school?

As you look ahead
to college and ultimately finding a career you enjoy, know there are many different paths that you can take.

But they all have one thing in common: most require some kind of education or training after high school.

Which path works for you?

Just by getting an education after high school, you’re setting yourself up for success. But the path you take can be your own — there are many ways to get an education after high school. Maybe you’ll go straight to a four-year college and then on to graduate school, or perhaps a four-year degree will set you on your way. Maybe you’ll choose to start at a community college, and then transfer to a four-year school for your final two years. Or perhaps a trade or technical college is the path to your chosen career.

Different factors might drive your choice: your passion for a specific career, your goal to save money, your dream of going to a certain school, your desire to explore different career options, and more. Deciding what’s important to you is the first step in shaping your experience after high school.

Things to consider as you make your choice

As you consider what educational path you’ll follow after high school, here are some things to think about as you make your choice:

Earning potential and job opportunities

Generally speaking, the more education you get, the higher your earning potential. In fact, any education beyond high school increases your earning potential significantly. The chart at the bottom of this page illustrates just how much your earning potential increases with each level of education.

As you think about the career path you’ll choose, consider the job opportunities that will be available to you, as well as the long-term earning potential your education can provide. You may want to take a look at jobs that are expected to be in growth fields in the next decade. The Bureau of Labor Statistics provides such a list with this information at www.bls.gov/ooh/fastest-growing.htm .

If you’re looking for additional resources to help determine what career path might be a good fit for you, turn to Tuition Funding Sources. By visiting tuitionfundingsources.com , you can learn more about various career options from day-to-day job tasks to earning potential.

Job security and satisfaction

While earning potential is one thing to consider when choosing your career path, there are other important factors as well, such as job security and satisfaction. As economic conditions change, you want to be in a career that can withstand those changes.

Getting a solid education is a start — college graduates have greater job security than those without a degree. The unemployment rate is just 3.5% for those with a bachelor’s degree, versus a rate of 6.0% unemployment for those with only a high school diploma.1 Satisfaction in your career is another important consideration, and college graduates seem to have an edge in that department as well, seeing themselves on a career path, not just working in a job.2

Borrow wisely

While college is a good decision, it’s still important to be sensible about paying for it. If you plan to borrow for college, it’s critical that you make sensible choices and only borrow what you need.

When taking out student loans, the amount you borrow should stay in proportion to your earning potential, so you’re in a better position to successfully repay your loan. We provide worksheets that can help you match up future earnings against the amount borrowed. (See the “Are you borrowing sensibly?” callout below.)

JUNIOR YEAR

Junior checklist

Junior year is all about setting yourself up for success during your senior year, when things really start to come together for college. As a junior, here’s what to focus on:

Challenging coursework

Be sure you’re taking challenging classes that will prepare you for college. Consider taking advanced placement or even college courses if you have the opportunity.

Scholarship possibilities

Consider opportunities that will help position you well for scholarships. This includes not only getting good grades or performing well in extracurricular activities, but also community involvement or other demonstrations of leadership. Figure out where you can shine — it may translate into a scholarship down the road.

College choices

Consider which colleges you want to attend. Spend some time narrowing down your list of potential schools, and try to end up with a list of 3 – 6 that you’d like to visit.

College visits

In the spring, you’ll want to start visiting the campuses you’re interested in. Try to visit when classes are still in session, if possible. Before you take a campus tour, check out the campus tour information you received when you signed up for the CollegeSTEPS program.

ACT/SAT preparation

Look into preparatory classes you can take through your school or check out the test prep materials that are offered online Plan to take these tests in the spring of your junior year. More details

It’s senior year! While this is a great year to create lasting memories with friends, why not also help each other out as you’re planning for college? Although each person’s journey is different, here’s a list you can follow planning toward college together:

College admission

Transcripts

SECOND SEMESTER
START HERE:

Financial aid

Advanced Placement (AP) tests

If you’ve taken AP courses, be sure to register to take the exams at the end of the semester. If you score well, you may be able to earn college credit.

College selection

If required, mail in your tuition deposit by the deadline in order to secure your admission. Contact the school’s financial aid office to make sure your application is complete.

Orientation

Register and mark your calendar for summer orientation at the college or university you selected. If you’ll be registering for classes at orientation, it’s a good idea to attend an earlier session for a better selection.

Wrap up

Write thank you notes to anyone who wrote you a letter of recommendation for college or a scholarship.

Celebrate graduation

Be sure to take time to celebrate your high school graduation with family and friends.

Topics to talk about with
your school counselor

The closer college gets

The closer college gets, the more questions you’ll likely have. There are a number of people you probably turn to often for helpful information, such as your parents, older siblings, friends and teachers. While those individuals are good to turn to, there’s one more person you should add to the list: your high school counselor.

While thinking through your path after high school, it’s a good idea to talk it over with your high school counselor. Your school counselor may be able to offer

insight, advice, and resources that you hadn’t thought about. Plus, it can be helpful to get the perspective of someone outside your circle of family and friends.

High school counselors typically have a lot of information and resources that can help you make decisions about your future, so you can ask them any questions about college planning and future career aspirations. Set up a time to chat and come prepared with a list of questions. Here are a few topics to help get you started:

Comparing colleges

If you’re looking at several different schools, it can be a challenge to sort them out. Your school counselor is a great person to help with this, by bringing an objective viewpoint, knowledge of your achievements in school, and awareness of what it takes to submit an application and be accepted at different schools. With the experience of working with many students over the years, your school counselor is in a good position to help you find a school that fits your academic goals and your personality.

Find scholarship opportunities that fit YOU

Tuition Funding Sources provides access to over 7 million scholarships totaling more than $41 billion to help you pay for college. Create your personal profile and begin your search at tuitionfundingsources.com .

With the experience of working with many students over the years, your school counselor is in a good position to help you find a school that fits your academic goals and your personality.

Applications and recommendations

When you’re completing college applications, your school counselor can help you through the process, from brainstorming essay topics to helping you manage your deadlines.

Another key component of your college application might be a letter of recommendation. Your school counselor can help with things like who to ask, how to follow up, and more.

Scholarships and financial aid

Discovering ways to pay for college is a big part of your journey during your senior year.

As you look for ways to cover your costs, scholarships should be at the top of your

list, since it’s money that doesn’t have to be repaid. Your school counselor can help with resources for finding scholarships that may be available from local organizations, the college you’re interested in, or other sources. If you’ll be borrowing money for college, your school counselor can help you through the financial aid process as well.

Exploring majors and more

Even if you’ve got your college choice and your financial aid plans all wrapped up, you may still have questions about possible majors and determining which path fits you best. Your school counselor can help by talking through your academic strengths and personal interests. Your school counselor may also have

assessment tools that can help guide you toward a career path that fits your personality. Planning for and heading off to college is a big deal. It’s normal to have questions and concerns about major life transitions like going to college and thinking about your future, so be sure to look to your school counselor for help whenever you need to talk.

Q&A: ACT and SAT tests

You’ve probably been hearing about them for years, but what do you know about the ACT and SAT tests?
Check out the Q&A below to find out more.

There are a number of different ways to prepare

Q. How do I prepare for the ACT/SAT?

A. There are a number of different ways to prepare for the ACT or SAT test. Check to see if your school offers a preparation class. There are also books you can purchase or check out from a library to help you study and free practice tests you can take online. Find some of these resources at act.org. Additionally, look for free apps to help you prepare such as ACT question of the day (Android) and ACTStudent (iOS). For SAT prep, check out The Official SAT Questions of the Day (iOS and Android) or SAT Up (iOS).

Q. Should I take one of these tests?

A. Consider the path that you’re planning after high school to determine if you should take the ACT or SAT. While it may not be a requirement, depending on the school you’re attending, consider taking the long-term view when making the decision. You may be planning to attend a school or program that doesn’t require the ACT or SAT now, but if your plans change down the road and you need these scores for admission, you may be very happy you already took the tests.

Q. When should I take the test for the first time?

A. Typically, students will take ACT or SAT in the spring of their junior year, because it gives them time to retake the test in the fall if they choose.

Find out the test dates by visiting actstudent.org. or
sat.collegeboard.org. sat.collegeboard.org.

Q. What’s the difference between the ACT and the SAT tests?

A. While the ACT and SAT are both standardized tests that can help you get into college, they do differ a bit from each other. The ACT is focused on achievement, measuring the bigger picture of what you’ve learned in school, while the SAT measures aptitude, testing your reasoning skills and verbal abilities.1

For more details on how the ACT and SAT tests differ, visit The Princeton Review at princetonreview.com/sat-act.aspx.

Q. How many times can I take the test?

A. It’s generally advised to take the test no more than three times, to avoid raising red flags with college admissions counselors. Prepare for the test as though you’re only going to take it one time, and do your very best. If you’re not satisfied with your score you can take it again.

Tips for
getting the most
out of your
college classes

Heading off to college often means a lot of exciting changes, like moving to a new place, meeting new people, and living on your own. But in the midst of that, it’s also important to remember that college is a big investment and the main reason you’re there is to do well in your classes and get a great education.

Heading off to college often means a lot of exciting changes


College graduates see the value

While college may be a significant cost for many students, research shows that most college graduates see its benefit. In fact, 86% of people with a college education feel they’ve already seen the value of their college education.1

Making the most of your college education

Given that college is such an important decision, how can you make the most of it? Preparing in high school by taking advanced placement or college courses, being active in the community, or participating in extra-curricular activities can help students start college on the right track. Taking rigorous math, science, and writing courses can also help.

Smart study habits

As many students quickly realize, college will be different from high school. Your schedule may seem very light at first — you may only be in class for a few hours each day. The catch is that your professors expect a lot of you outside of class. There is much more reading, studying, and homework to do than you may be used to from high school.

Because you’ll have so much work to do on your own, you don’t just want to study hard, you’ll want to study smart. One important way to do that is to match your studying approach to your learning style. By now, you probably know what helps you learn things best — do flashcards work for you? Or are you better off discussing the material with a study partner? Does it help you to draw or map out concepts? Whatever tactics you’ve found that work for you, keep using them.

Another key component to successful studying is to find the right environment. Are you easily distracted by others? Find a quiet corner of the library. Do you thrive on working with a hum of activity around you? Study in the student center. Have trouble sitting still? Work out before you settle down to study, or plan to concentrate for shorter bursts and take frequent breaks.

The most important thing is to figure out what lets you get the most out of your study sessions. Find out what helps you learn and go with it!

More tips to keep you on track:

  • Go to class and actively participate. Nothing makes up for missing a lecture, not getting notes from a friend, or only reading your textbook. Catch the live version and make it a point to always show up for class. In a large lecture hall, sit near the front if possible — you’ll better hear what the professor is saying and you’re less likely to be distracted. When you actively participate in class you’ll gain a better understanding of the material and build a better rapport with your professor at the same time.
  • Review your notes frequently. Take thorough notes during class, and review them frequently as part of your study routine. Don’t wait until the night before a test to take a look at your notes — review them as part of your reading assignment for class.
  • Do the reading. College reading is often assigned in chapters, not pages. It may be difficult to catch up and retain the information if you don’t keep up with your assigned reading.
  • Organization and time management. It’s critical to stay organized and manage your time well during college. Find the place where you do your best studying and plan a specific time to be there to get started. Keep all your study materials handy, so you’re ready to get started when the time comes.
  • Sync up your study habits with the ways you learn best. If you’re a person who needs peace and quiet to study, study as much as possible during free time between classes. Stay on campus, stake out a spot in the library, and do your work — free from distractions. If you’re restless when studying, plan a time and place to study where you can take frequent breaks to get outside, or plan your study sessions to directly follow a workout at the gym. Whatever your learning style, plan your study sessions to accommodate it.
  • Avoid all-nighters. While it may seem like a college student’s rite of passage, you don’t have to pull all-nighters studying for tests and working on projects. Use the tips in this article to stay on top of your work and get your rest.

Creative hacks
to enhance your study space

Let’s face it, you’ll probably spend a fair amount of time studying in your dorm room during college. If you want to make some fun enhancements without spending a lot of money, sometimes you just have to get creative.

Check out these four useful hacks to help improve your room’s study space in college:

Check out these four useful hacks

  1. Hack #1 Get organized. Keep things orderly in your dorm room with a cool bookshelf made out of plastic milk crates and zip ties.

  2. Hack #2 Be more efficient. When you’re studying hard, you don’t need a bad internet connection slowing you down. You can boost your Wi-Fi signal using a simple soda can.

  3. Hack #3 Reward yourself. Everyone needs a little motivation to help them through hours of studying. Try using gummy bears to reward yourself as you read through long chapters. These chewy treats will break up a long study session and take you right back to the playground.

  4. Hack #4 Take a study break. Sometimes it feels great to put down the books for a few minutes and crank up some music to get your mind off things and blow off some steam. You can get some serious volume out of your little phone speakers with just a 2-liter soda bottle and a pair of scissors. A couple of snips and you’ve got an instant mini dance party!
