What students should know

before heading to college

Helping your students learn the skills they’ll need.

Your student has been preparing for college one way or another since the first day of school. But school doesn’t necessarily cover everything students should know before heading off to college and establishing their independence.

As a parent, you play a key role in helping your student with the practical and life skills necessary to be successful in college. Check out the following list to see if your student needs a crash course in any of these basic skills.

Academics

Your student has likely been focused on taking the right classes and getting good grades, but even if they have this under control so far, there are still additional skills they need to master as they head off to college.

Good study habits. Some students are able to get by without studying much during high school — but college is a different story. Encourage your student to develop strong study habits during high school and emphasize how important they’ll be in college. Talk about minimizing distractions, building in study breaks, the advantages of reviewing notes frequently, and how to manage their time when completing big projects or studying for big tests.

Solid note-taking skills. Keeping up with professors can be a challenge in college. But it’s an important skill to learn, since taking good notes during class is key to doing well. Talk with your student about how they take notes now, and suggest some strategies for coming away from class with a solid set of notes. If you aren’t sure how to help, suggest getting ideas from their teachers about the best way to capture the important ideas from a lecture.

Choosing classes wisely. Taking advanced placement classes can help students get college credit while they’re still in high school. If it seems like a good fit, encourage your student to take advantage of this opportunity. It will allow them to save money and time during college. Even if they’re able to test out of just one class, it can lighten their course load during that first year and allow them more time to focus on other classes.

Financial

When students need money for college, it’s important for them to understand all the details of their financial aid options, including loans, and what their responsibility is to repay. Get your student engaged with paying for college by going over their award letter together, talking through the details of what it means to them, and whether or not any additional funding is needed.

Evaluating tuition costs. First things first. Every student should know exactly what their tuition costs and what it includes. It can help give them some understanding and appreciation of the value of their education, and of completing their degree in a timely manner.

Understanding award letters. When your student’s award letter arrives, look it over together and help your student understand what aid they’re being offered to cover their costs. If your student will be borrowing student loans to help pay for college, be sure to discuss the different types of loans available and which type makes the most sense for your student and family.

Learning about student loans. Help your student get a handle on the details of student loans if they need financing to cover college expenses. Learning the facts about student loans is a great first step to understanding the responsibility of borrowing only what they need, taking thoughtful care of their expenses during college and preparing them to repay their loans once they’ve completed their degree.

Creating a budget. As you begin to discuss what their finances might look like, it’s a great time to start talking about how to manage their money while they’re in college. Every college student should understand how to track their spending to create a budget. Getting control over their money is a life skill that will serve them throughout college and beyond. You can get the conversation started with helpful tools found on our website at wellsfargo.com/goals-going-to-college/student-budget.

Practical

If your student has been more focused on studying and school activities than doing housework, well, they’re not alone. But before they leave for college it’s a good idea to teach your student some practical skills they may need when they’re out on their own.

Mastering the laundry. Have your student practice a few loads of their own before heading off to college. If they don’t know the basics of separating colors and whites, and simple stain removal techniques, that’s a good place to start. Maybe taking on their own laundry duties for awhile would help them practice and gain some independence before leaving for college.

How to cook. If your student will be cooking while at college, it’s a must to get the basics down before leaving for school. Put together some simple recipes and let them get some practice. Maybe include some bigger meal ideas as well that they could freeze and eat during the week.

Basic car maintenance. Most high school students don’t do much car maintenance, so if your student is taking a car to school, a few lessons might be in order. Learning how to check the oil, put air in the tires, jump start a dead battery, change a flat, and add washer fluid are just a few basics that they’ll be glad to know when the time comes. An emergency kit for the trunk also makes a great graduation gift.

Your help and guidance are likely needed as your student prepares for college, so plan to team up on this college-planning journey. As a person just on the cusp of adulthood, your student brings dreams and plans for the future to the table — as a parent, you provide years of life and financial experience to help send them on their way.

A parent’s role in college financing

As a parent, you play an important role in helping your student finance college. Whatever path your student has chosen after college (four year college, community college, technical school, etc.), you can help them understand what that choice means — financially speaking — and provide support by pointing them in the right direction.

Whether you’re helping your student understand their financing options, cosigning a private student loan, or borrowing a parent loan to help pay expenses, your role matters.

  • Encourage your student to apply for scholarships. You can help out by checking to see if there are scholarships available through your employer or organizations you’re involved in. You can also visit Tuition Funding Sources and search over 7 million scholarships totaling more than $41 billion to help your student pay for college at tuitionfundingsources.com.
  • Remind your student to complete the FAFSA after January 1, because it’s required in order to receive most types of financial aid. If your student is a dependent, you will both need to complete the form. You can do so by visiting fafsa.ed.gov.
  • If your student’s education expenses exceed the amount of financial aid they receive, you may be able to help cover this financial gap. Determine your role, if any, by considering being a cosigner or taking out a loan for the benefit of your student. As a cosigner, you assume equal responsibility for the student loan and it becomes part of your credit profile. If you choose to take out a loan in your own name, there are both federal and private student loan options available.
  • Take action to ensure that your credit is in good shape before it’s time to borrow. If you’re cosigning a private student loan, a better credit score for you might mean a lower interest rate for your student. If you are borrowing a parent loan, a better credit score could also mean a lower interest rate for you. For more tips on how to manage your credit, visit wellsfargo.com/financial-education/credit-management.

Parents’ planning guide

As a parent, there are a few different ways you can lend a hand to your student during the college-planning process. From helping find scholarships, to reviewing award letters together, your role is important.

Here’s a checklist to help you get started:

Scholarships

Check whether there are scholarships available through your workplace or organizations to which you belong. You can also encourage your student to visit Tuition Funding Sources for access to 7 million scholarships totaling more than $41 billion to help pay for college: tuitionfundingsources.com.

Complete applications and secure references

Help your student create a way to organize the application and reference-gathering process.
Suggest potential references.
Emphasize the importance of following up with a thank-you note for each person that was a reference.

Take the ACT or SAT

If your student needs to take or re-take these tests, help ensure he or she is registered and ready to go.

Apply to schools

Together, meet with the financial aid offices at any schools your student has applied to — in order to better understand their process, costs, and expectations.

Apply for financial aid

Together, complete the FAFSA as soon as possible after January 1 for each academic year your student plans to attend college.
Learn more about the financial aid process by reviewing Your financial aid journey in 5 steps, which can be found at wellsfargo.com/fivesteps.

Review award letters

Along with your student, carefully review any award letters received. Help your student determine what aid to accept and what may not be needed.

Celebrate

Graduation day is a big achievement. Celebrate your student’s accomplishment together!

Learn the basics of federal and private student loans

Financing options to help pay for college.

There are a number of different loan options available for students and parents who plan to borrow money to cover college expenses. These loans fall into two main categories: federal student loans and private student loans. Each type of loan has unique characteristics with different repayment terms and conditions. Understanding the differences can help you decide which loan or combination of loans will meet your family’s needs.

Student loan options

Financing options

Federal Perkins Loans are for undergraduate and graduate students with exceptional financial need.

Federal Direct Loans (subsidized and unsubsidized) are available to undergraduate and graduate students attending college at least half-time.

Federal Direct PLUS Loans are credit-based loans offered by the federal government. These loans are available to graduate or professional degree students and parents of dependent undergraduate students.

Private or alternative loans are credit-based student loans which may be available for graduates, undergraduates, professional degrees, or qualified certificate or licensure programs. There may also be financing options for anyone (such as a parent, relative, or sponsor) who may be interested in borrowing to help the student pay for college. These loans are provided by banks or other lenders.

Student loan costs

Before taking out a student loan, it’s important to carefully review and understand the total cost of each, including the interest rate and fees.

Interest rates for federal student loans are set by Congress. All new federal student loans have a fixed interest rate. Find current federal rates at studentaid.ed.gov/types/loans/interest-rates. The interest rates for private student loans vary and are based on criteria determined by the lender. The most common criteria are creditworthiness and income.

Depending on the lender, borrowers may be able to choose whether to have a fixed or variable interest rate on their private student loans. Variable rates may change over the life of the loan, while fixed interest rates remain the same.

Fees are costs you have to pay to originate or service your loan. These are charges you pay in addition to interest. If you take out a federal or a private student loan, keep in mind the provider may deduct the fees directly from the original loan amount — so the amount you receive in your loan disbursement may appear to be less than the amount you actually borrowed. However, you are still required to repay the full amount you borrowed, not just the amount you received in your disbursement.

Student loan repayment terms

Repayment is another important factor that should be carefully researched before taking out a student loan.

Term is the length of time you have to repay your loan. Before you take out a loan, determine how long you will have to pay it back and whether there are any penalties for early repayment.

You’ll want to find out if there is a grace period for the loan before repayment begins, so you and your student can plan for when the loan repayment actually begins.

Additionally, it’s important to examine the full payment schedule for any loan you’re considering. Many times people only look at the estimated payment schedule based on the first year of borrowing. To get a more realistic idea of the total monthly payments, consider the total amount of student loans borrowed for your student to complete a degree.
